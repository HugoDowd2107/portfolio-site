Nourish checkout redesign

Summary

I led the redesign of the checkout experience for Nourish, a food delivery platform serving 2M+ users. The existing multi-step checkout had high abandonment rates and was costing the business significant revenue. By simplifying the flow and introducing smart defaults, we dramatically improved conversion and customer satisfaction

Impact of the redesign

34% reduction in cart abandonment during checkout

The streamlined single-page checkout removed unnecessary friction and kept users engaged through to order completion

22% increase in average order value

Smart upsell placement and a visible order summary encouraged users to add more items before completing their purchase

NPS improved from 42 to 67 within three months

Post-launch surveys showed users found the new checkout significantly easier and faster to complete

Checkout was the biggest point of failure in the user journey

68% of users who added items to their cart did not complete an order

Analytics showed the majority of drop-offs happened between the cart and order confirmation screens, indicating the checkout flow itself was the problem rather than product selection

Users were abandoning at the delivery details step

The largest single drop-off point was the address and delivery time selection screen, where users were required to fill in 7 separate fields before proceeding

Competitive pressure from faster alternatives

Competing platforms had moved to one-tap reorder and streamlined checkout flows, making Nourish's 4-step process feel dated and slow by comparison

Why checkout experience matters for food delivery

Time sensitivity

Food delivery users are often hungry and ordering in the moment. Every extra second in checkout increases the likelihood they will give up or switch to a competitor

Delivery trust

Users need confidence that their food will arrive when expected. Unclear delivery estimates during checkout erode trust and lead to cancellations

Repeat behaviour

A smooth checkout creates a positive last impression that drives repeat orders. Food delivery relies heavily on habitual usage and a frustrating checkout breaks the habit loop

Research methods

Overview

We combined usability testing of the existing checkout with semi-structured interviews and diary studies to understand the complete ordering journey

What we wanted to understand

  • Where in the checkout flow users were getting confused or frustrated
  • What information users needed at each step to feel confident about their order
  • How experienced users differed from new users in their checkout behaviour
  • What role saved preferences and past orders played in the experience

Research revealed three core pain points

summary

Through usability testing and interviews, we identified three main areas where the checkout experience was failing users. These became the focus of our design efforts

Users couldn't see the full picture

The multi-step flow meant users couldn't see their delivery details, order summary and total cost at the same time. This created anxiety about whether they had entered everything correctly

Delivery time estimates felt unreliable

The estimated delivery time was only shown after completing the address step, and users reported it often changed after they entered payment details, eroding trust

Returning users had to re-enter information

Despite having ordered before, users were required to confirm their address and payment details every time. Power users found this especially frustrating

Mapping the existing checkout showed unnecessary complexity

summary

The existing flow required 4 separate screens and 14 interactions to complete a single order. For returning users with saved details, this could be reduced to just 3 interactions

Existing flow

  • Cart review screen
  • Delivery address entry
  • Delivery time selection
  • Payment and confirmation

Key issues identified

  • No persistent order summary
  • Redundant confirmation steps
  • No smart defaults for returning users
  • Delivery estimate shown too late

Design direction

summary

Research pointed to three design principles that guided the redesign: reduce steps, show everything upfront and remember the user

Three design principles

  • Consolidate the checkout into a single scrollable page so users always see the full picture
  • Show delivery estimates and order totals in real-time as users make selections
  • Use smart defaults and saved preferences to minimise input for returning users

Single-page checkout

summary

The 4-step checkout was consolidated into a single scrollable page with a persistent order summary sidebar

Why a single page

  • Users can see all their information at once, reducing the anxiety of hidden details
  • Edits can be made in-place without navigating back through steps
  • The persistent order summary keeps the total visible, reducing surprise at the final screen

Progressive disclosure for complexity

While all sections are on one page, advanced options like delivery instructions, tip selection and promo codes are collapsed by default. This keeps the primary flow clean while still providing access to the full feature set

Smart defaults for returning users

Address autocomplete and saved locations

Returning users see their most recent delivery address pre-filled. New users get address autocomplete powered by geolocation, reducing the address entry from 7 fields to a single search input

Remembered payment methods

Saved payment methods are shown as selectable cards rather than requiring re-entry. The most recently used method is pre-selected

One-tap reorder

For users reordering from the same restaurant, the entire checkout can be pre-filled based on their previous order, reducing the flow to a single confirmation tap

Real-time delivery estimates

summary

Delivery estimates now update live as users adjust their address, order size and delivery time preference, building trust throughout the checkout

Dynamic time calculation

As soon as a delivery address is entered, the estimated delivery time appears and updates in real-time based on restaurant prep time, driver availability and distance

Time slot selection

Users can choose between ASAP delivery or scheduled time slots. Slots that are unavailable due to restaurant hours or driver availability are greyed out with a clear explanation

Design challenges

Balancing information density on mobile

  • The single-page approach risked feeling overwhelming on smaller screens
  • We used collapsible sections and a sticky order total bar to keep the experience manageable
  • Usability testing confirmed mobile users preferred scrolling over stepping through pages

Handling payment failures gracefully

  • Payment errors in the old flow sent users back to step 1, losing all their input
  • The new design keeps all fields intact and highlights only the payment section with a clear error message
  • Users can switch payment methods without re-entering any other information

Accommodating promotional complexity

  • Promo codes, loyalty rewards and restaurant offers all needed to be visible but not dominant
  • A collapsible "Offers" section was added below the order summary that auto-expands when applicable offers are detected

Outcomes

summary

The redesigned checkout launched to 100% of users after a successful A/B test with 20% of traffic. All key metrics showed significant improvement within the first quarter

Checkout completion rate increased from 32% to 66%

The single-page design with smart defaults cut the number of users abandoning during checkout by more than half

Average checkout time reduced from 2m 40s to 48s

Address autocomplete and saved preferences meant returning users could complete checkout in under 30 seconds

Customer support tickets for checkout issues dropped 60%

Clearer error handling and real-time validation reduced user confusion and the need to contact support for failed orders

Learnings

summary

This project reinforced several important design principles and revealed areas where we could improve our process for future work

Progressive disclosure is essential for complex forms

Putting everything on one page only works when you thoughtfully hide complexity. Without collapsible sections and smart defaults, the single-page approach would have been worse than the original

Test with real orders, not hypothetical scenarios

Our most valuable usability insights came when we asked participants to place their actual lunch order during testing. Hypothetical ordering tasks missed the urgency and decision-making that drives real behaviour

Returning users and new users need different experiences

Early prototypes treated all users the same. The breakthrough came when we designed two distinct paths: a full checkout for new users and an express checkout for returning users with saved details
